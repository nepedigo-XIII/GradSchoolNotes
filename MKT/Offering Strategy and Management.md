# **1. Foundations of Offering Strategy**
An organization’s profitability depends fundamentally on its *offering*—the product or service exchanged with customers.

## **1.1 Role of the Offering in Marketing**
- The offering is the **unit of exchange**.
- Without an offering, there is nothing to:
  - Price  
  - Distribute  
  - Promote  
- Acts as the **centerpiece of the marketing mix** (value creation).

## **1.2 Basis for Offering Decisions**
Offering decisions must consider:
- **Customer Needs** → Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning (STP)
- **Competitive + Environmental Forces** → 5 Forces, PESTL
- **Internal Capabilities** → VRIN resources
- **Strategic Alignment** → Mission, objectives, competitive strategy

---

# **2. Product as a Value-Creation Mechanism**
A product is a **bundle of benefits** rather than a physical item.

### **2.1 Dimensions of Product Value**
The product provides value in four domains:
- **Economic**
- **Functional**
- **Experiential**
- **Social**

### **2.2 Extended Product Considerations**
Value also depends on:
- Core functionality  
- Ease of use  
- Ease of installation  
- Warranty  
- Brand reputation  
- Post-sale service  

> *The core function alone rarely makes the sale—value-added attributes matter.*

---

# **3. Modifying the Offering Mix**
Because markets, buyers, and competitors evolve, the offering mix must also evolve.

## **3.1 Why Offerings Must Change**
Driven by:
- Competitor actions  
- Shifts in buyer preferences  
- Need for organizational growth  

## **3.2 Forms of Additions**
1. **New product lines** (increase mix breadth)
2. **New items within an existing line** (increase depth)

---

# **4. Evaluating New Offering Opportunities**

## **4.1 Market Considerations**
- Does a viable market exist? (willingness + ability to buy)
- Does the product offer a **competitive advantage**?
- Does it address a **distinct, unmet segment**?

## **4.2 Resource Considerations**
- Financial strength  
- Ability to fund R&D and marketing  
- Cash outlays before profit (time to break even)  
- Market growth rate  
- Expected **competitive response**  

## **4.3 Strategic Considerations**
- Will the new offering confuse the current target market?
- What are the **demand interrelationships**?
  - Substitutes → risk of cannibalization  
  - Complements → strategic synergy  
- Fit with:
  - Communication strategy  
  - Distribution strategy  
  - Brand identity (avoid dilution)

---

# **5. Types of Offering Additions**

## **5.1 Product Innovation**
- Radical / system-level innovations  
- “New to the world” or “new to the firm”
- High technological + market uncertainty  
- Often **technology-push**

## **5.2 Product Augmentation**
- Incremental improvements to current products  
- Bundling, performance enhancements  
- Lower uncertainty  
- Often **need-pull**

## **5.3 Product Line Extensions**
- Variations in size, features, form, flavor, etc.  
- Designed to broaden appeal or sharpen segmentation  

---

# **6. Offering Life Cycle (OLC)**

A Life Cycle plots **sales** of a product or product class over time.

## **6.1 Life Cycle Stages**
1. **Introduction**
2. **Growth**
3. **Maturity/Saturation**
4. **Decline**

Sales depend on:
- Number of **triers**
- Number of **repeaters**

### **Sales Formula**

Sales = (Number of triers × average purchase amount × price)
      + (Number of repeaters × average purchase amount × price)

---

# **7. Stage 1: Introduction**

## **7.1 Characteristics**
- Sales driven primarily by **trial purchases**
- High promotional and informational effort required

## **7.2 Marketing Priorities**
- Advertising to build awareness and knowledge  
- Distribution expansion for availability  
- Promotion incentives to encourage trial  

## **7.3 Communication Objective**
**Primary Demand** → Educate customers about:
- Benefits of the offering class  
- Overcoming objections  

---

# **8. Stage 2: Growth**

## **8.1 Characteristics**
- Competitors enter  
- Most sales driven by **repeat purchases**

## **8.2 Marketing Priorities**
- Increase usage among current customers  
- Encourage trial among new potential users  
- Take customers from competitors  
- Strengthen brand communication and distribution  
- Competitive pricing (may rise or fall)  
- Line extensions & product modifications  
- Avoid pitfalls:
  - Cannibalization  
  - Brand dilution  
  - Confusing product lines  
  - Excessive line length (cost of variety)  

### **8.3 Stretching & Filling the Line**
- **Upward Stretch**  
  Add higher price/performance items  
- **Downward Stretch**  
  Add lower-cost items  
- **Line Fill**  
  Add a product between existing items to:
  - Close gaps  
  - Preempt competition

---

# **9. Cannibalization vs Draw**

## **Cannibalization**
Degree to which a new product takes sales from the firm’s existing products.

## **Draw**
Degree to which the product:
- Steals customers from competitors, or  
- Attracts new category users  

A strategically successful launch maximizes **draw** and minimizes **cannibalization**.

---

# **10. Stage 3: Maturity–Saturation**

## **10.1 Characteristics**
- Market is dominated by **repeat purchasers**
- Few new triers remain
- Products become standardized
- Heavy pricing and promotional pressure

## **10.2 Marketing Strategies**
- Increase usage frequency  
- Identify new customer segments  
- Improve offerings through radical innovations  
  (to restart the life cycle)

---

# **11. Stage 4: Decline**

## **11.1 Strategic Options**
1. **Harvest**
2. **Eliminate (Divest)**

## **11.2 Harvesting Strategy**
Reduce investment to:
- Cut costs  
- Improve cash flow  

Appropriate when:
- Market share becomes expensive to defend  
- Stable market with loyal customers  
- Offering contributes to brand image  
- Product helps maintain a full product line  

## **11.3 Eliminating the Offering**
Drop a product if answers to the following are “little or none”:
- Future sales potential  
- Contribution to profitability  
- Support for other offerings  
- Potential gains from modifying the offering  
- Impact of removal on channels and customers  

---

# **12. Transitioning Innovations to the Mass Market**
Key insights:
- Early adopters’ preferences strongly shape long-term development  
- Superior alternatives may be abandoned prematurely  
- Consumers may resist change due to behavioral biases  
- Customers often cannot articulate needs until they see a product  
  *(“Customers don’t know what they want until they see it.”)*

---

# **13. Key Takeaways**
- Offering strategy is central to firm competitiveness  
- Products must evolve to align with markets, competition, and firm capabilities  
- Product life cycle understanding ensures targeted communication and investment  
- Cannibalization must be managed strategically  
- Mix decisions must align with STP, brand architecture, and resource constraints
