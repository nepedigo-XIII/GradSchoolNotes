## **Architectural Innovation**
-A product innovation process that combines familiar components or existing technologies in an innovative arrangement to target new markets.

## **Blue Ocean Strategy**
- Strategy aimed at creating new market spaces or modifying existing ones to create demand rather than competing.
- Examples: Disney, Cirque du Soleil, Apple (iPod/iPad), Tesla.
- Characteristics: demand creation, linking technology to buyer value, can emerge from core business, strong brand building.
- Formula: BO = Where Company Competencies meet Customer Needs.

## **Value Innovation**
- Core concept of blue ocean strategy: pursue differentiation and low cost simultaneously.
- How it works: reduce or eliminate industry factors to lower cost, raise or create new value to increase demand.

## **Four Actions Framework**
- Framework to guide value innovation by asking four key questions:
  - **Eliminate:** What should be removed?
  - **Reduce:** What should be reduced below industry standards?
  - **Raise:** What should be increased above standards?
  - **Create:** What new factors should be introduced?

[Four Actions Framework](FourActionsFramework.jpg)

- Become a rule breaker.

## **Customer Value Drivers**

## **Diversification Strategy Subtypes**
- Related Diversification refers to the expansion into industries with strategic fit, while Unrelated Diversification refers to expansion into industries with no strategic connection. 

- Related-linked Diversification: Less than 70% of a firm's revenues come from a single business and some other business activities share linkages to the main business focus while others do not.

- Related-Constrained Diversification: Executives only pursue strategies where they can apply the existing resources and capabilities of the parent.

- Product Market Diversification Strategy: Firm is active in several product markets across several geographic areas.

- Product Diversification Strategy: Firm is active in several product markets within a geographic area. 


## **Economies of Scale**
- Cost advantages that arise from producing at larger volumes.

## **Economies of Scope**

## **Four Ways of Thinking Strategically**
- Analytical Thinking.
- Second-Level Thinking.
- 180-Degree Thinking (Who doesn't know this?).
- Systems Thinking.

## **Second-Level Thinking**
- Strategic thinking focusing on hidden patterns and unconventional insights.
- Involves synchronicity and creative insight.

## **Systems Thinking**
- Strategic thinking focusing on "why" questions and holistic organizational understanding.
- Application to organizations and individuals for understanding roles and purposes.

## **Analytical Thinking**
- Focus: "How" questions.
- Breaks problems into components.
- Best for efficiency and red ocean strategies.
- Weak for creativity and innovation.

## **180-Degree Thinking**
- A mindset that involves intentionally adopting the opposite perspective, often that of a competitor or critic  
- Encourages holding contradictory ideas simultaneously to uncover deeper insights and alternative strategies  
- Requires low ego, openness, and comfort with ambiguity, enabling more innovative and unconventional solutions  

## **TOWS Analysis Framework**
- A strategic planning tool that extends a traditional SWOT analysis by focusing on matching internal factors (Strengths, Weaknesses) with external factors (Opportunities, Threats) to generate actionable strategies  
- Emphasizes four strategy types:  
  - SO (Strengths–Opportunities): Use strengths to capitalize on opportunities  
  - WO (Weaknesses–Opportunities): Overcome weaknesses by leveraging opportunities  
  - ST (Strengths–Threats): Use strengths to avoid or mitigate threats  
  - WT (Weaknesses–Threats): Defensive strategies to minimize weaknesses and avoid threats  
- More action-oriented than SWOT because it forces strategic pairing and decision-making, not just analysis  

## **Synchronicity**
- The occurrence of meaningful coincidences that appear related but lack a direct causal connection  
- Often interpreted as patterns or connections that emerge unexpectedly and provide insight or direction  
- In strategic or creative thinking, it can help individuals recognize hidden relationships or opportunities others overlook
